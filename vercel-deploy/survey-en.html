📎 Appendix — for HRBP / program designers

How to use TNA results to decide the workshop design flow

After collecting responses, open the "Live Results" board (results.html) to compare Question 2 (knowledge) with Question 6 (interest) per topic — it automatically averages scores and updates continuously.

Decision principles

  • If the proficiency gap between individuals is large (some are very new, some already proficient) across most topics → design by PROFICIENCY LEVEL: split into Beginner / Intermediate–Advanced tracks, with a shared foundation session before splitting groups.
  • If individual proficiency is fairly even, but scores differ clearly between topics (e.g. everyone is weak at Prompt Engineering but fine at writing emails) → design by TOPIC: consecutive sessions per application topic, with the whole group learning together.
  • If both factors vary significantly → design a HYBRID: 1 shared foundation session (to align knowledge + expectations) + topic-based modules, each with exercises split into 2 difficulty levels (basic / advanced) so everyone participates together but practices at their own level.

Cross-reference Knowledge (Q2) with Interest (Q6) per topic to prioritize content

  • High interest – Low knowledge: prioritize adding to the program early (real need, easy to motivate).
  • High interest – High knowledge: consider inviting them to share a case study / mentor the group.
  • Low interest – Low knowledge: clarify the benefits / concrete use cases before technical training.
  • Low interest – High knowledge: leverage them as a champion, no need to prioritize further training.

Also cross-check with Question 10 (self-assessed group) to confirm the above decision fits the group's actual preferences, before finalizing the workshop flow.
